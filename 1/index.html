Part 1: Selfie — The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Take a picture of a face from close and far distance.
My analysis. After matching face size, in the close shot, the camera is near the nose but farther from the ears and cheeks, so relative distances to different parts of the face vary a lot. This geometry exaggerates depth, making a larger nose and larger eyes distance in the sefie. When the character step back and zoom in, the absolute subject distance increases and all facial features are at more similar distances from the center of projection. That yields a more natural-looking portrait.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Take a picture of an urban scene from close and far distance.
My analysis. With the far-away, zoomed view, the camera is distant from both near and far elements, so their relative distances to the camera are more similar. That reduces the rate at which size diminishes with depth, making the scene look “flatter” (parallel lines converge more slowly; background buildings appear larger than expected). When we move forward and use a wider angle to keep framing similar, the near elements are now much closer than the distant ones. The larger distance ratio amplifies perspective cues (stronger size falloff, faster line convergence), so the scene feels deeper.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Duplicate the classic film effect, dolly zoom.
My analysis. The dolly zoom keeps the subject’s image size approximately constant while changing the camera’s position and focal length in opposite directions. As we dolly back and increase focal length, the background appears to expand toward the subject (compression decreases), producing the classic “Vertigo shot”.
